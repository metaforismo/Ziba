# The Name

Ziba comes from *zibaldone*, and more specifically from Giacomo Leopardi's
*Zibaldone di pensieri*.

A zibaldone was not a tidy system. In Italian, the word carries the sense of a
mixture: first a dish made from many ingredients, then a miscellany, then a
notebook where facts, excerpts, reflections, sketches, and unfinished thoughts
could gather as they arrived. Treccani records that passage from mixture to
manuscript, and it is exactly this movement that made the word feel right for
the project.

Leopardi began his *Zibaldone* in Recanati in the summer of 1817, when he was
nineteen, and continued it until December 1832. It grew to 4,526 pages. It was
private, immense, and alive: a place where philology, philosophy, poetry,
memory, language, science, literature, and self inquiry could meet before they
became finished works. Treccani presents it as a vast field of subjects and as
the record of a thought still in motion. The University of Birmingham, which
helped bring the first complete English translation to readers in 2013,
describes it as the primary laboratory of Leopardi's thinking.

Ziba is named after that kind of book.

Not after disorder for its own sake. Not after nostalgia for paper. Rather after
the human act of keeping a living reserve of thought: fragments that are not yet
essays, notes that may become arguments, readings that need a place to echo,
projects that begin as a line and later become structure.

In Leopardi, the zibaldone is not a passive archive. It is a workshop. It
accepts mixture, then lets relation, repetition, contrast, and time produce
meaning.

That is also the ambition of Ziba. A vault should let a person collect freely,
but it should not trap thought in a pile. Plain markdown gives permanence.
Typed objects give shape. Relations give direction. Graphs give distance.
Search gives return. The system should help thought remain open while still
becoming navigable.

The short name, Ziba, keeps the root and loses the weight. It is lighter than
the full word, easier to say, and still connected to its origin. It suggests a
modern tool with an old intuition behind it: knowledge begins as mixture, but it
becomes useful when we can revisit it, connect it, and make it speak again.
